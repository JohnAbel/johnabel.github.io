Research focus: mathematical foundations of circadian rhythms

The circadian oscillator is a biological control system designed to regulate gene expression in a 24-hour periodic environment. The gene regulatory network comprising the circadian oscillator may be modeled through systems of coupled chemical reactions. Mathematically, these reactions may be described as a set of coupled nonlinear ordinary differential equations, or in some cases, as a probabilistic chemical master equation. I use mathematical modeling and statistics tools inside an empiricist framework to better understand circadian phenomena.

There are two foci within my work. First, I study how different organisms generate oscillation within individual cells, coordinate those oscillations across tissues or populations, and how signals confer information about the temporal environment. I additionally study the development algorithms and techniques for medical manipulation of circadian processes. For a list of relevant publications, please see my CV.

Research focus: the anesthetized brain

General anesthesia is a state of unconsciousness, analgesia, and amnesia that is routinely induced for many medical procedures. My postdoctoral research is concentrated on understanding the brain under anesthesia, especially during aging. I am developing statistical, machine learning, and controls approaches for guiding anesthesia and improving clinical practice. For a list of relevant publications, please see my CV.

The majority of this work is performed in collaboration with the rest of the Neuroscience Statistics Research Laboratory at MIT and Massachusetts General Hospital. I additionally collaborate with Balachundhar Subramaniam at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center.
